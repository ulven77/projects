# Life Companions

Malik is not a solitary figure by nature. His companions define the stages of his life. Some travel with him; some stay behind; some die and become data points in his journal. All of them are loved.

---

## Brother Aldric — The Namer (Stage 2)

**Who:** A traveling monk-scholar. Natural philosopher. Not a practitioner.

**Role:** The first adult who sees Malik clearly and responds with *curiosity* rather than fear or piety. Gives Malik his intellectual framework. Does not stay.

**Arc:** Passes through, leaves books, leaves a framework, leaves a direction. Malik never sees him again. Late in life he finds Aldric's grave and sits with it for a long time.

**What he represents:** The gift of being named correctly. How much a single encounter can redirect a life.

**Death:** Natural, years before Malik finds the grave. The question of whether Malik considers restoring him — and decides not to — is a key late-book moment.

---

## The Apothecary's Apprentice — The First Peer (Stage 3)

**Placeholder name:** Sera

**Who:** Apprentice to the town apothecary. No magic. Excellent analytical mind. Reads Galen the way Malik reads Aristotle — as a living text, not a closed authority.

**Role:** His first intellectual equal. She asks *why* when he says *how*. She pushes back. She is the first person he tells voluntarily.

**Arc:** They work together through his early scholarship years. She becomes a full apothecary. She is unwilling to travel — she has her own practice, her own people. They stay in contact by letter for decades. She dies of old age while he is still middle-aged. He is at her bedside.

**Key scene:** He offers, once, to anchor her. She refuses. *"You love me enough to let me go. Do that."* He does.

**What she represents:** The first friendship between equals. Love that doesn't try to fix the beloved. Grief accepted rather than reversed.

---

## Master Ezra — The Protector (Stage 4)

**Who:** Professor of natural philosophy at the university. Older. Brilliant. Quietly heretical in his own right — he believes the Church's position on soul-science is politically rather than spiritually motivated.

**Role:** Academic patron and cover. Writes letters on Malik's behalf. Absorbs institutional blowback. The closest thing to a father figure in Malik's adult life.

**Arc:** Stays at the university his entire life. Protects Malik's thesis and reputation long after Malik leaves. Dies in his chair, surrounded by books, at a great age. Malik restores him briefly, just long enough to say goodbye properly — with Ezra's knowledge and consent, arranged years in advance.

**Key scene:** Ezra's pre-arranged restoration — the most ethically clean resurrection in the book. Both men are at peace. Ezra says something that becomes the epigraph of Malik's treatise.

**What he represents:** Institutional courage. Choosing the truth over safety. How rarely the two conflict if you're careful.

---

## The Inquisitor — The Mirror (Stages 5–7)

**Placeholder name:** Roan

**Who:** Not a villain. A Church Inquisitor who is genuinely, methodically convinced that what Malik does is wrong — not politically, but cosmologically. He believes soul-anchoring disrupts something essential about the order of creation.

**Role:** Antagonist, but the good kind — he is right about some things. He is wrong about others. Neither man can fully acknowledge this.

**Arc:** Pursues Malik for thirty years. Catches him twice; both times Malik escapes by circumstances rather than violence. In old age they meet deliberately, at Malik's invitation, for a single conversation that neither of them wins.

**Key scene:** The final conversation. Roan is dying — natural causes. He does not ask to be restored. He does not need to ask Malik not to. The silence between them is full. Malik sits with him until the end.

**What he represents:** The legitimate critique. The possibility that Malik is wrong in ways he can't see. The cost of being so right about the mechanism that you can't hear questions about the purpose.

---

## The Last Companion — The Witness (Stages 6–8)

**Placeholder name:** Davi (or Davia)

**Who:** Met in Stage 6. A young person who sought Malik out after hearing his reputation — not for power or healing, but because they want to *understand* what he understands. Possibly has minor Sight talent.

**Role:** The last person who travels with him. Present for the lich transition. The one who writes down what happened so it isn't lost.

**Arc:** Younger than Malik by thirty years. Becomes his archivist, assistant, and the person he trusts completely. Does not want to become a necromancer. Does want to understand. The tension between those two things drives their dynamic.

**Key scene:** Davi asks, the night before the anchoring, if Malik is afraid. He thinks about it honestly. *"No. But I'm sad. Those aren't the same thing."*

**What they represent:** The continuity of knowledge. The student who doesn't repeat the teacher. The witness who makes something real.

---

## The Dead Who Speak (all stages)

Malik keeps informal relationships with the recently-dead through Binding. Some of these conversations are brief; a few become extended. These unnamed figures constitute a kind of chorus throughout the later books — voices from the corridor of death, reporting back.

**Key recurring figure:** An old shepherd from Malik's village who dies when Malik is thirty. Malik speaks with him briefly out of grief and nostalgia. The shepherd is unfrightened and matter-of-fact about being dead. He becomes Malik's baseline reference: *how does ordinary death feel?* Malik checks in for years after, until the shepherd's soul finally disperses and there's no one left to answer.
