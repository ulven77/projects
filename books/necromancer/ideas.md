# Ideas to Explore

## Magic System Questions

- **What does the system ledger actually *is*?** Three candidate theories worth developing: (1) it's a natural law like gravity — impersonal, always there, only perceptible to sensitives; (2) it's a created artifact — something a prior lich or god built and it persisted; (3) it's a collective phenomenon — the emergent record of every practitioner who ever used spiritus, accumulated into a structure. Each theory has different implications for the lich ending.

- **What happens to the soul during a Binding consultation?** Is the dead person fully present? Partially? What does it feel like from their side? The book should answer this — probably through a scene where Malik is briefly on the verge of death himself and experiences it from the inside.

- **Can spiritus be transferred between living practitioners?** If yes, what does that mean ethically? Voluntarily donating mana to Malik so he can save someone is very different from him taking it.

- **What is the Church's actual magical theory?** They should have one — they're not just ignorant. They have their own framework for soul mechanics (theological rather than natural-philosophical) and it's internally consistent. Where does it overlap with Malik's science? Where does it diverge in ways that actually matter?

- **The Animation Tree moral question:** Guided Corpse and Shell Walker require no soul — just physical manipulation of dead tissue. Is this different from surgery? Is a corpse that walks on command a desecration or a tool? Malik's position evolves.

- **Can you restore someone who doesn't want to be restored?** The consent question. Malik's answer at age 8 is different from his answer at age 40. The book should have at least one scene where this question is live.

## Scenes to Write

- **The caterpillar, adult Malik:** He encounters another dying caterpillar at age 50. He sits with it. He has the power to restore it easily. He doesn't. He watches it die and records everything. This scene has no dialogue. It's the midpoint of the whole story.

- **Malik teaches:** He has students at various points. A teaching scene where he explains necromancy to someone who is afraid of it. What does he say? How does the explanation change between Stage 4 and Stage 7?

- **The plague village:** The moment he works openly for the first time. Mass casualty event. He saves people who should be dead. Exhausts himself completely. What does a village look like when half the people who were dying are standing up again? What do the people who didn't make it feel?

- **Roan's final conversation:** Thirty years of pursuit. Both men are old. Neither is performative anymore. What do they actually say to each other? This scene should be the best argument *against* what Malik has done — presented fairly, by someone who means it.

- **The first failure:** Malik at 16, trying to restore a drowned child. He fails. He knows, from this moment, that there is a ceiling problem and he will spend his life solving it. What does it feel like to have power and have it not be enough?

- **Davi writing it down:** The final chapter or epilogue, from Davi's POV. They are writing the account of the lich transformation. They don't fully understand what they watched. They describe it precisely and honestly and admit what they don't know. This is the book's last voice.

## Thematic Threads

- **Entropy as the antagonist, not evil.** Malik is never fighting a dark lord. He's fighting thermodynamics. This is rare in fantasy and worth leaning into fully.

- **The cost as clarifying, not punishing.** In most LitRPG, cost is a constraint to be overcome. Here, the cost is *informative* — every unit of spiritus spent tells Malik something about what he's doing. The system is not a barrier; it's the instrument.

- **What grief is allowed to do.** Most necromancer stories are about grief that won't let go. Malik's story inverts this: he does let go (Sera, the shepherd, eventually everyone). The lich transformation is not about holding on — it's about having enough time to understand. Grief is the engine, not the destination.

- **Legitimacy vs. practice.** At every stage, Malik's practice is technically illegal or heretical, and at every stage it is clearly beneficial. The book asks: what are institutions for, if not to protect the practice they're suppressing?

- **The naturalist's dilemma:** If you understand something completely, do you inevitably affect it? Malik's presence changes death in his vicinity — not just by reversing it, but because the dying can sense him. He is a variable in the system he's studying.

- **What the lich is paying *for*:** The book's central question. Not answered until the last chapter. Candidates: knowledge (to keep writing, keep understanding); protection (one or two people who are still alive and need him); witness (to see the world change in ways he set in motion); completion (there is one more thing he needs to understand before he lets go).

## Structural Ideas

- **Each stage as a book:** Eight stages = potential eight-volume series. Or compress stages 1–3 into Book 1, stages 4–5 into Book 2, etc.

- **The journal as framing device:** Each chapter opens with an entry from Malik's field journal — terse, scientific, dated. The entry is about whatever creature or case he's currently studying. The chapter then shows the human story behind the clinical note.

- **System box evolution:** In early chapters, system boxes are fragmentary — Malik barely perceives them, they're garbled. As his understanding grows, the boxes become more precise, more legible. In the lich stage, he can read the system so clearly that his own notifications read like *he* wrote them.

- **The Codex chapters:** Occasionally the narrative pauses for a full Codex entry — as if we're reading from Malik's published treatise. These are in a different register (formal, third-person, retrospective) and give the reader framework without slowing the story.

## Open Questions (unresolved, for the author)

1. Does Malik ever restore a human against their will? If so, when, and how does he carry that?
2. Is Davi present at the lich transformation, or does Malik do it alone?
3. What does Roan feel in his final moments — does he feel Malik's Sight on him, and does he find it comforting or invasive?
4. Is the shepherd's soul's final dispersal depicted on the page? It should be.
5. What is Malik's relationship to his own body in old age — he's been spending spiritus on himself his whole life. Is he healthier than he should be? More aware of his own decline than a normal person?
6. Does the book have an ending beyond the lich transformation, or does it end at the moment of anchoring? If Davi writes the epilogue, how much time has passed?
